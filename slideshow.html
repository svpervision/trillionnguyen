05
Sidebar Redesign The Challenge

The Challenge.

The app's original navigation lived in a floating action bar fixed to the top of the screen. While it kept navigation accessible, it created persistent tension with the content below — overlapping key UI elements, interrupting scroll behavior, and making the layout feel cluttered as the product matured.

The floating pattern that once felt lightweight had become a liability. The goal was to rethink navigation from the ground up — moving away from the floating bar and toward a collapsible sidebar that integrates cleanly into the layout and gives users control over how much space navigation takes up at any moment.

"The shift from a floating bar to a sidebar wasn't just a layout change — it was a fundamental rethink of how navigation should coexist with content."

06 Sidebar Redesign Discovery

Research.

Discovery started with session recordings and user interviews to understand why the floating bar had stopped working. A clear pattern emerged — users were frequently scrolling past it, accidentally triggering it, or losing track of it in content-heavy views.

We conducted a competitive analysis across Notion, Linear, and Figma — studying how leading products handle persistent navigation, how collapse behavior is communicated, and how state is preserved across sessions.

07
Sidebar Redesign User Pain Points

User Pain Points.

  • 01Scrolling past the bar — users frequently lost the nav when moving through content-dense views
  • 02Accidental triggers — reaching for content beneath the nav bar caused unintended interactions
  • 03Content overlap — the floating bar obscured key UI elements, creating visual and functional clutter
  • 04No layout memory — users had to re-configure their workspace preference every session
08
Sidebar Redesign Information Architecture

Information
Architecture.

The sidebar needed to support three distinct states, each serving a different user intent without breaking the mental model between them.

  • ExpandedFull sidebar with labels — icons and text visible, all nav items accessible at a glance
  • CollapsedIcon rail only — minimal footprint, navigation awareness preserved through iconography and tooltips
  • HoverFlyout label overlay — collapsed icon rail triggers a contextual flyout, no layout shift required

State transitions needed to feel instant and controlled — never jarring. Persistence across sessions meant the sidebar "learned" the user's preference from the first interaction.

09 Sidebar Redesign Prototyping

Explorations.

A clickable prototype was built early to validate the collapse interaction before investing in high-fidelity design. The prototype focused on trigger mechanics — where users expected to click, how the transition felt, and whether the icon rail was sufficient for navigation in the collapsed state.

Stakeholder walkthroughs with PMs and engineering surfaced early feasibility questions around animation performance and state management. These shaped the handoff spec before a single line of production code was written.

10
Sidebar Redesign Design Decisions

Design Decisions.

  • 01Fixed sidebar anchored left — removes float/content tension permanently, integrates into layout
  • 02Collapse toggle at the top — clear, discoverable affordance that gives users explicit layout control
  • 03Icon rail in collapsed state — preserves navigational awareness without visual noise of the original bar
  • 04Tooltips on icon hover — resolved confusion in collapsed state without breaking the minimal aesthetic
  • 05Session-persisted collapse state — the app remembers the layout each user prefers, eliminating repetitive setup
11 Sidebar Redesign Design

Final Solution.

The redesign moved navigation off the floating layer and into a fixed sidebar anchored to the left. A persistent collapse toggle at the top gives users direct control over their layout — reducing to an icon-only rail when space is needed, expanding fully when context matters.

An edge handle in the collapsed state offers a low-friction re-entry point. Flyout labels on hover preserve clarity without requiring the sidebar to open fully.

12
Sidebar Redesign Impact

Impact.

Moving from a floating action bar to a collapsible sidebar resolved the core tension users had been experiencing — navigation no longer competed with content for space. The collapse feature saw strong adoption post-launch, and users consistently noted feeling more in control of their workspace layout.

The icon rail approach proved effective in the collapsed state, preserving navigational awareness without the visual noise of the original floating bar. The design also leaned heavily on the existing design system — reusing tokens, motion values, and component patterns — keeping the build lightweight and the experience consistent with the broader product.

"Navigation no longer competes with content. Users feel in control of their workspace for the first time."

13
Sidebar Redesign Reflection

Reflection.

The most impactful change turned out to be the smallest one: persisting collapse state per session. Users don't want to re-configure their workspace every time they open a product. State persistence is a form of respect.

The most technically complex part — replacing the entire nav paradigm — was also the most straightforward in terms of user reception. The users who had struggled with the floating bar immediately understood the sidebar. Good architecture makes good UX easier.

"The difference between a good tool and a great one is often memory. Does it remember who you are?"

14 Noteworth Product Design · Mobile · Healthcare · 2019–2020

Goals &
Action Plan

RoleProduct Designer
CompanyNoteworth
Year2019–2020

An accordion-based goal-setting system letting clinicians define patient goals and actionable steps — built for mobile-first workflows in a healthcare context where clarity isn't optional.

Full Case Study →
15
Noteworth The Challenge

The Challenge.

Clinicians faced a tedious, error-prone process of manually setting patient goals. This resulted in unreliable data, incomplete information, and lengthy processing times — all of which had direct consequences for patient care quality.

There was no structured way for clinicians to define goals with actionable steps, or for patients to suggest goals and report challenges back to their care team. The system needed to work seamlessly across both web and mobile with the same underlying data model.

"The key challenge was prioritizing information in a limited viewing window without sacrificing the depth clinicians needed to do their jobs."

16 Noteworth Discovery

Research.

Competitor analysis revealed that form builders and accordion patterns offered real potential for streamlining complex, nested data entry without overwhelming users with all fields at once.

Clinician workflows were mapped end-to-end — from goal creation to patient follow-up — to identify where the existing process broke down and what a digital system needed to handle that paper-based workflows had absorbed informally.

17
Noteworth User Pain Points

User Pain Points.

  • 01Clinicians: manual, freeform goal entry with no structure — data was incomplete and inconsistent across patients
  • 02Clinicians: no way to attach actionable steps to a goal — the "what next" was left to memory and paper
  • 03Patients: no mechanism to suggest goals or flag challenges back to their care team within the app
  • 04Both: the mobile experience was an afterthought — desktop-first design created friction for patients on the go
18
Noteworth Information Architecture

Information
Architecture.

Goals needed a clear hierarchy that could scale to unlimited entries without overwhelming the interface. The accordion model made that possible — collapsing inactive goals to expose only what matters in the moment.

  • Level 1Goal — top-level item with title, status flag, and expand/collapse control
  • Level 2Action Steps — nested tasks beneath each goal, each with its own completion state
  • Level 3Notes / Feedback — patient-reported updates and clinician annotations attached to each step

The same data model had to render correctly across web (clinician) and native mobile (patient) — different surface areas, same information hierarchy.

19 Noteworth Explorations

Explorations.

Early explorations tested a flat-list approach before moving to the accordion. The flat list made it easy to see all goals at once but created serious scroll depth problems at scale — clinicians managing patients with 8–12 active goals couldn't work efficiently.

The accordion immediately reduced cognitive load by surfacing only the active goal while keeping others within reach. It also mirrored how clinicians already thought about patient progress — one goal at a time, with supporting context available on demand.

20
Noteworth Design Decisions

Design Decisions.

  • 01Accordion over flat list — collapsible goals reduce visual overload while keeping all entries accessible
  • 02Unlimited goals + nested steps — mirrors real clinical workflow without artificial constraints
  • 03Mobile-first for patient-facing views — patients interact on native mobile, clinicians on web; same data, different surface
  • 04Above-fold prioritization — most critical actions visible without scroll — respects the small mobile viewport
  • 05Flag system for urgency — priority and status visible at a glance without opening the goal details
21 Noteworth Design

Final Solution.

An intuitive accordion-style form empowers clinicians to create unlimited goals and nest actionable steps — breaking down goals into manageable steps that foster patient engagement and clear follow-through.

The space-saving solution translates seamlessly to the mobile app, ensuring a smooth experience for patients on the go. Comprehensive documentation was produced to ensure consistent implementation across engineering, mobile, and web.

22
Noteworth Impact

Impact.

The Goals & Action Plan feature enabled personalized healthcare at scale — clinicians could create tailored programs specific to each patient's needs, and patients could conveniently record and report their progress directly through the app.

Enhanced collaboration between patients and clinicians fostered by this feature led to better treatment adherence and outcomes. The structured data model also made patient progress trackable over time, giving clinicians the longitudinal view they previously had no way to access digitally.

"The right interface can meaningfully improve the quality of care. That's not an abstraction — it's the job."

23
Noteworth Reflection

Reflection.

Healthcare is a context where clarity isn't optional — a confused clinician means a worse outcome for a patient. The constraint of a small mobile screen forced every decision to be ruthless. If it didn't need to be there, it wasn't.

That discipline made the final design better than any desktop-first approach would have. Starting from the most constrained surface — the patient's phone — and expanding upward to web produced a system that was clear everywhere, rather than one that was spacious on desktop and cramped on mobile.

"The best constraints produce the clearest design. The mobile screen forced every decision."

24 Design System Design Systems · Tokens · Components · 2021–2022

Design
System

RoleLead Product Designer
ScopeTokens · Components · Documentation

Built and led adoption of a scalable design system from the ground up — from token architecture to cross-team implementation across multiple products.

25
Design System The Challenge

The Challenge.

There was no design system in place — leading to fragmented design patterns, lack of consistency, low brand recognition, and time-consuming design and development processes.

Every team was making independent decisions. The product had grown in multiple directions without a shared foundation. The main challenge was creating a system usable across multiple products while maintaining consistency and significantly speeding up design and development.

"A design system isn't a Figma library. It's a shared language — and building one means getting everyone to speak it."

26 Design System Discovery

Research.

An audit of existing designs uncovered the extent of the inconsistency — multiple versions of the same component across different products, no shared color system, and typography applied differently by every team.

User journey mapping across all products highlighted where inconsistency created real friction for end users — not just designers. The research made the case internally that this wasn't a design preference problem; it was a product quality problem.

27
Design System User Pain Points

User Pain Points.

  • 01Design teams were rebuilding the same components from scratch for each new feature — no reuse, no shared starting point
  • 02Engineering handoffs were inconsistent — specs didn't agree on spacing, color values, or interaction behavior across products
  • 03End users experienced a fragmented product — the same action felt different depending on which part of the app they were in
  • 04New designers had no reference for how the product should look and behave — every decision started from zero
28
Design System Information Architecture

System
Architecture.

The system was structured in composable layers — each built on the one below, making it extensible without breaking what already existed.

  • Layer 01Foundations / Tokens — color, typography, spacing, motion, elevation defined as named variables
  • Layer 02Components — atoms (button, input) → molecules (form field) → organisms (modal, nav) built from tokens
  • Layer 03Patterns — page-level templates and interaction patterns composed from components
  • Layer 04Documentation — usage guidelines, do/don't examples, and accessibility notes living alongside every component
29 Design System Explorations

Explorations.

Before designing a single component, I established overarching design principles and a unified token system for color, type, and spacing. This gave every subsequent component decision a clear reference point and prevented the same divergence from re-emerging.

The modular approach was tested by applying early components to real product screens — surfacing edge cases and usage patterns that refined the system before any team-wide adoption began.

30
Design System Design Decisions

Design Decisions.

  • 01Token-first architecture — visual decisions as named variables, not hardcoded values — changes cascade automatically
  • 02Modular components — customizable within bounds, not rigid templates — teams retain flexibility without breaking consistency
  • 03Documentation alongside assets — usage guidelines built into the component library, not separate; adoption requires education
  • 04Workshops over announcements — teams needed to learn by doing, not by reading a PDF
  • 05Multi-product testing before v1 — edge cases surfaced early, before any team was committed to a broken pattern
31 Design System Design

Final Solution.

A complete library of reusable components built on a unified token foundation — with comprehensive documentation explaining each component, its usage guidelines, and real product examples.

The system was implemented across multiple products simultaneously, with feedback loops built into each stage. Quality improved while design and development time decreased significantly — freed from repetitive component work, the team could focus on higher-level product thinking.

32
Design System Impact

Impact.

Quality improved across all products while design and development time decreased significantly. Reusable components and a streamlined process led to major efficiency gains — new features that previously required days of design work could be assembled in hours.

Users experienced a seamless and familiar journey across all products, enhancing brand perception and reducing support friction. The design system became the cornerstone of the design process — transforming fragmented experiences into a cohesive, efficient ecosystem.

"Freed from repetitive component work, the design team could focus on what design is actually for — solving the right problems."

33
Design System Reflection

Reflection.

A design system is a product for designers and engineers. It has users, requires maintenance, and needs an adoption strategy as much as it needs good design. The hardest problem wasn't building the components — it was convincing teams to stop making exceptions.

Consistency is a discipline. Design systems are the infrastructure that makes it achievable. But infrastructure only works when people trust it — and trust is built through workshops, documentation, and being willing to update the system when the system is wrong.

"You don't ship a design system. You grow one. It only works when teams believe in it more than their own workarounds."
